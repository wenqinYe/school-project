1920s Consumerism

The Summary!

The twenties were a time period of major economic and social boom for the western provinces of Canada. After the war, people wanted to forget about fighting and truly live life. Technological advancements coupled with changes in consumer spending habits--due to increased disposable income--led to the consumer culture that we all familiar with today. It was a time people did and bought things for pleasure rather than for necessity, and a person's status was derived from their material possessions rather than from what they did. This was a huge paradigm shift in consumer thinking that lasted until the Great Depression--when the boom ended.

Buy Me Right Now!

This poster is an advertisement for a Ford car in the 1920s with a fictional young man who is using a Ford car to run his own business. One interesting thing to note is that the young boy’s smiling in the poster insinuates that the use of the car makes him happy. Thus, the message of the poster is clear; buy a Ford car and you will get a sense of happiness every time you use it. This style of marketing, where advertisers try to convince consumers of the emotional feelings one will get from using the car, is reminiscent of the boom in consumerism during the roaring twenties and appeals to consumers’ sense of desire rather than logic.

The advertisement talks very little about the actual functionalities of the car. This is not an accident. Instead the poster talks about the pleasurable experience one will get from using the car. There was a huge paradigm shift in people’s buying habits; people starting to buy things for pleasure rather than need. The twenties were a time of huge increases in disposable income, and so people had money to spend on enjoying life, instead of just living life. Thus, this meant buying things that they may not have necessarily needed. Marketers during the roaring twenties exploited this change in consumer attitude to appeal to the consumer’s desires rather than their logic. In the ad there are no discussions about the features of the car or how it works.

This source is reliable because it comes from the Henry Ford museum, which is a well known museum dedicated to providing historical information on Henry Ford. The audience for the car were for adults who might want to buy their sons a car. The information in the poster is likely to be exaggerated as its main purpose was to convince consumers to buy a Ford car; thus, one should not take the information in the poster as fact, but should instead look at the overview of the message conveyed by the poster.
