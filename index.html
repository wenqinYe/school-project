All Play and No Work

The twenties are also known as the roaring twenties--and for good reason. The twenties were a time period of major economic boom for the western provinces of Canada. After the war, people wanted to forget about fighting and truly live life. Technological advancements coupled with this change in thinking of the consumer led to the consumer culture that we all familiar with today. People in the twenties started to buy things for pleasure rather than for necessity. Furthermore, marketers changed from advertising the features of their products to touting the emotinal experience one will get using their product; thus, creating desire with the consumer.

Average worker's salary for production workers by the end of the year increased by 10%, while average office worker's salary increased by 20%. Furthermore, average worker's annual salary in Canada increased from just $2,366 to $2,940 in just 4 years (from 1926 to 1930).

As the salaries of worker's increased, so did the fun. With extra money people could finally buy things for pleasure rather than necessity. this led to the boom in consumerism during the twenties, where buying in ever greater quantities and enjoying life was deemed the most important thing.

Paradigm Shift

The twenties was a period of a huge paradigm shift in consumer thinking. People thought started to buy products for wants instead of needs. One's status shifted from what they did to what they owned. Marketers took note of this and marketered their products as desirable things to consumers, often times touting the emotional experience of the product rather than how the actual product worked. This contrasts to how product marketing before the twenties was all about describing the features of the product, and the consumer uisng logic to determine whether or not they needed the product. Thus, buying was the result of gut feeling rather than logical thinking of whether or not the consumer needed product.
